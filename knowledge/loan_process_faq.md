## Part 1: VA Loan Basics

### Q: What is a VA loan and who offers them?
A VA loan is a mortgage loan guaranteed by the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs and
issued by private lenders (banks, credit unions, mortgage companies). The VA does not
make loans directly — it guarantees a portion of each loan, which reduces the lender's
risk and allows lenders to offer favorable terms (no down payment, no PMI, competitive rates).

### Q: Do I need a down payment for a VA loan?
**No** — eligible Veterans with full entitlement can purchase a home with **0% down
payment**, regardless of the purchase price. There is no loan-to-value cap when a
Veteran has full entitlement.

Veterans with remaining (partial) entitlement may be required to make a down payment
if the loan amount exceeds their remaining entitlement's coverage. The required down
payment is calculated as:
```
Down Payment = (Loan Amount – Entitlement × 4) × 25%
```

### Q: Is there mortgage insurance (PMI) on a VA loan?
**No** — VA loans never require private mortgage insurance (PMI), regardless of the down
payment or loan-to-value ratio. This is one of the most significant financial benefits of
the VA loan program. Conventional loans require PMI when the LTV exceeds 80%; VA loans
eliminate this cost entirely.

### Q: What is the VA Funding Fee and can it be waived?
The VA Funding Fee is a one-time fee (ranging from 0.50% to 3.30% depending on the loan
type and prior use) paid at closing. It funds the VA loan guarantee program and eliminates
the need for PMI.

The fee **can be waived entirely** for:
- Veterans receiving VA disability compensation for any service-connected rating
- Active duty Servicemembers who have been awarded the Purple Heart
- Surviving spouses of Veterans who died in service or from service-connected disability

Funding fee exemption must be verified through VA records before the fee is waived —
it is not self-certified by the borrower.

## Part 2: Eligibility and Entitlement

### Q: Can I use my VA loan benefit more than once?
**Yes** — VA loan benefits can be used multiple times. There is no limit to how many
times a Veteran can use their VA loan benefit over a lifetime, as long as entitlement
is available or restored.

**Ways to use VA benefits again:**
1. **Sell your home and pay off the VA loan** — entitlement is automatically restored
2. **Have your entitlement formally restored** — VA Form 26-1880; requires the prior loan
   to be paid in full (even if property is not sold)
3. **Use remaining entitlement** — if you have a current VA loan but have remaining
   entitlement, you may be able to take a second VA loan simultaneously
4. **Assumable loans** — if a qualified Veteran assumes your existing VA loan, your
   entitlement is restored

### Q: I'm still paying on my first VA home. Can I get another VA loan?
**Possibly yes.** If you have remaining entitlement, you may be able to take a second
simultaneous VA loan. The amount depends on:
- The county loan limit where you're buying
- How much entitlement was used on the first loan
- Whether the new loan amount stays within the coverage of your remaining entitlement

**Example**: If your first VA loan used $100,000 of entitlement, and the new county's
conforming limit is $806,500, you may have up to $101,625 in remaining entitlement,
supporting a no-down-payment loan of up to ~$406,500 on the new property.

If the new purchase price exceeds the coverage of remaining entitlement, a down payment
equal to 25% of the excess is required.

### Q: I'm a Reservist — am I eligible for a VA loan?
**Yes**, if you meet service requirements. National Guard and Reserve members are eligible
after:
- 6 years of service in the Selected Reserve or National Guard, OR
- 90 days of active duty under Title 10 orders (including at least 30 consecutive days)

Reserve members who were called to active duty under Title 32 orders for national
emergency purposes may also qualify — their eligibility should be reviewed on a case-
by-case basis with the VA Eligibility Center.

### Q: My discharge was "Other Than Honorable" — am I eligible?
**Possibly.** An OTH discharge does not automatically disqualify a Veteran. The VA
reviews OTH discharges individually through the VA Eligibility Center. Relevant factors
include the circumstances of the discharge, length and character of service, and whether
a Discharge Upgrade has been granted by the relevant military branch.

Veterans with OTH discharges should submit VA Form 26-1880 and request an eligibility
determination. This process can take several weeks to months.

## Part 3: The VA Loan Homebuying Process

### Step 1: Confirm Eligibility and Obtain COE
- Verify service requirements are met
- Obtain Certificate of Eligibility (COE) through lender's VA portal, VA.gov, or by mail
- Check for VA funding fee exemption (if applicable)

### Step 2: Get Pre-Approved
- Select a VA-approved lender
- Provide financial documentation (pay stubs, W-2s, bank statements)
- Lender issues a pre-approval letter with loan amount and rate estimate
- Pre-approval is not a guarantee of final loan approval

### Step 3: Find a Property and Make an Offer
- Work with a real estate agent experienced with VA transactions
- Identify properties that are likely to meet VA Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs)
- Include a **VA Escape Clause** in your purchase contract (required by VA): allows the
  Veteran to exit the contract without penalty if the appraisal comes in below purchase price

### Step 4: VA Appraisal
- Lender orders a VA appraisal through the VA's appraisal roster (not lender-selected)
- VA appraiser assesses value **and** MPR compliance
- Timeline: typically **7–14 business days** in most markets; longer in rural areas
- Appraised value becomes the maximum loan amount for VA purposes

### Step 5: Underwriting
- Lender reviews all documentation, appraisal, and title
- VA may issue Automatic (AU) or Prior Approval (PA) designation
  - **Automatic**: Lender-approved VA lenders issue final approval without VA review
  - **Prior Approval**: Loan package submitted to VA Regional Loan Center for review
- Underwriting timeline: **3–10 business days** typically

### Step 6: Clear to Close and Closing
- All conditions are satisfied; lender issues Clear to Close (CTC)
- Final loan documents prepared; closing scheduled
- At closing: Veteran signs documents, pays closing costs (or uses lender credits),
  and receives keys
- Occupancy certification signed: Veteran intends to occupy within 60 days

### Typical VA Loan Timeline (Purchase)
| Phase | Typical Duration |
|---|---|
| Pre-approval | 1–3 business days |
| Home search | Varies |
| Appraisal | 7–14 business days |
| Underwriting | 3–10 business days |
| Closing | 1–2 business days after CTC |
| **Total (from contract to close)** | **30–45 days** (typical) |

## Part 4: Common Myths and Misconceptions

### Myth: "VA loans take longer and sellers won't accept them."
**Not true** (mostly). In competitive markets, VA offers are sometimes perceived as
slower due to the appraisal process. However, a well-prepared VA borrower with a
pre-approval and an experienced lender typically closes in the same 30–45 day window as
a conventional loan. Many sellers and listing agents are fully comfortable with VA offers.

**Buyer tip**: Ask your agent to proactively communicate with the listing agent about
your VA pre-approval strength and the lender's VA experience.

### Myth: "I can only use my VA loan benefit once."
**False.** VA loan benefits can be used repeatedly throughout a Veteran's lifetime.
Entitlement resets when a prior VA loan is paid off, or can be restored through a formal
VA entitlement restoration request.

### Myth: "VA loans can't be used on condos."
**Partially true, with nuance.** Condos must be in a **VA-approved condo project** to be
eligible. VA-approved projects are listed on VA.gov. However, individual unit approval
(DELRAP) is also available in some cases. Not all condo complexes are VA-approved —
this is an important due diligence step when searching for a VA-financed condo.

### Myth: "The VA requires a perfect credit score."
**False.** The VA itself sets no minimum credit score. However, **lenders** set their own
credit overlays. Most VA lenders require a minimum FICO of 580–640. Veterans with scores
in that range may face higher rates or additional conditions, but are not automatically
disqualified from the VA loan program.

### Myth: "I have to pay all closing costs out of pocket."
**False.** VA loans have flexible closing cost options:
- **Seller concessions**: Sellers can pay up to 4% of the purchase price in concessions,
  plus all normal closing costs
- **Lender credits**: Borrower accepts a slightly higher rate in exchange for lender-paid
  closing costs
- **Roll-in (IRRRL only)**: Most closing costs can be financed into an IRRRL
- Veterans can and often do purchase with **$0 out of pocket** in the right circumstances

### Myth: "VA loans are only for buying single-family homes."
**False.** VA loans can be used for:
- Single-family homes (1 unit)
- Multi-unit properties (2–4 units, with owner occupancy of one unit)
- Condominiums (VA-approved projects)
- Manufactured homes (on permanent foundations; subject to additional requirements)
- New construction (VA has specific inspection requirements for new builds)

## Part 5: Edge Cases

### Deployed Borrower
A Veteran who is deployed or on active duty orders may still obtain a VA loan. Key points:
- **Power of Attorney (POA)**: A spouse or other designated person can sign loan
  documents on behalf of the deployed Veteran with a valid, lender-accepted POA
- **Occupancy**: Occupancy can be satisfied by the Veteran's spouse or dependents
  moving into the home if the Veteran cannot do so within 60 days
- **Lender communication**: Notify your lender of deployment status early — some lenders
  have VA deployment specialists on staff

### Divorce / Separation
- If a VA loan is being divided in a divorce and a non-Veteran spouse is awarded the
  home, the Veteran's entitlement remains **tied up** until the loan is paid off or
  assumed by a qualified Veteran
- A non-Veteran spouse **cannot** assume the VA loan obligation without a qualified
  Veteran co-borrower or a conventional refinance
- Divorce decree alone does not release VA entitlement — the underlying loan must
  be resolved

### Death of a Veteran Borrower
- **Surviving spouse** of a Veteran who died in service or from service-connected
  disability is eligible for a VA loan in their own right (not as co-borrower)
- If the Veteran died for other reasons, the surviving spouse is **not** independently
  eligible, but may remain on the existing VA loan as long as they were a co-borrower

### Appraisal Gap (Value Comes in Low)
If the VA appraisal value is lower than the agreed purchase price:
1. **Seller reduces price** to meet appraised value (most common resolution)
2. **Veteran pays the difference** in cash (allowed — the VA Escape Clause only protects
   against being required to buy; the Veteran may choose to proceed)
3. **Veteran invokes the VA Escape Clause** and exits the contract with deposit returned
4. **Reconsideration of Value (ROV)**: Veteran can request the VA reconsider the appraisal
   if there is documented evidence (comparable sales) supporting a higher value. ROV
   turnaround is typically 5–10 business days. Lender submits ROV on Veteran's behalf.

### Short Sale / Foreclosure History
A prior VA loan that ended in **short sale** or **foreclosure** affects the Veteran's
entitlement:
- **Short sale**: If the lender suffered a loss, the VA must be reimbursed before
  entitlement is restored. If no claim was filed, entitlement may be available.
- **Foreclosure**: VA typically requires a **2-year waiting period** from the date of
  foreclosure before a new VA loan is approved. Entitlement is not restored until any
  VA claim on the prior loan is repaid.

In both cases, the standard credit waiting periods imposed by lenders (typically
2–4 years) apply independently of the entitlement question.

### VA Loan + FHA Loan Simultaneously
A Veteran may hold a VA loan and an FHA loan at the same time, as long as each loan
meets the occupancy requirements for its respective program. The VA loan must be on a
primary residence; the FHA loan may be on a separate primary residence (e.g., if the
Veteran is moving and renting out their prior FHA-financed home while purchasing with VA).

### Non-Veteran Co-Borrower (Joint Loans)
A non-Veteran may be a co-borrower on a VA loan:
- **Spouse of the Veteran**: Fully acceptable; VA guarantee covers full loan amount
- **Non-spouse civilian co-borrower**: VA guarantee applies only to the Veteran's share
  of the loan (typically 50%); the lender accepts additional risk on the remaining share.
  Some lenders decline these loans — it is lender-by-lender discretion.
- Income and credit of both borrowers are considered in underwriting

## Part 6: Refinance FAQs

### Q: What is the difference between an IRRRL and a Cash-Out Refinance?
| Feature | IRRRL | Cash-Out Refi |
|---|---|---|
| Existing loan type | VA loan only | Any loan type |
| Cash at closing | Not allowed | Allowed |
| Appraisal | Usually waived | Required |
| Income verification | Usually waived | Required |
| Credit underwriting | Usually waived | Required |
| Occupancy | Prior (not current) | Current required |
| Funding fee | 0.50% | 2.15% / 3.30% |
| Best use | Lower rate, lower payment | Access equity for any purpose |

### Q: How soon can I refinance my VA loan?
For an **IRRRL**, the VA requires:
- At least **210 days** since the first payment due date of the loan being refinanced, AND
- At least **6 payments made** on the existing loan

For a **Cash-Out Refinance**, there is no VA-mandated seasoning requirement, but most
lenders (including Valor Home Lending) impose a **12-month** seasoning overlay.

### Q: Can I refinance out of my VA loan into a conventional loan?
Yes — a Veteran may refinance a VA loan into a conventional loan at any time if it makes
financial sense. Reasons a Veteran might do this:
- They want to rent out their VA-financed home and preserve VA entitlement for a
  future purchase
- Conventional rates or terms are more favorable in a specific situation
- They want to remove a co-borrower without VA entitlement complications

**Caution**: Refinancing out of a VA loan forfeits the VA loan protections (no PMI,
VA servicing standards) and may require PMI if LTV exceeds 80%.

### Q: Will refinancing affect my VA entitlement?
- An **IRRRL** or **Cash-Out Refi** of an existing VA loan does not change your entitlement
  usage — the same entitlement that backed the original loan backs the new loan
- Refinancing into a **conventional or FHA loan** frees up your VA entitlement for future
  use, but only after the VA loan is fully paid off and formal entitlement restoration
  is completed (if needed)

### Q: What documents do I need for an IRRRL?
The IRRRL is the most streamlined VA product. Valor Home Lending typically requires:
- Signed loan application (1003)
- 12-month mortgage payment history (to verify no 30-day lates)
- Homeowners insurance declaration page (current policy)
- Government-issued photo ID
- Most recent mortgage statement (to confirm existing VA loan details)
- VA funding fee exemption documentation (if applicable)

Note: Pay stubs, tax returns, and bank statements are **not required** for a standard IRRRL.
